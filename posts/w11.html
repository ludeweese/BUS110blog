Week 11 Journal Entry

Formula for Success

The message “Formula for Success” from Thomas S. Monson felt very meaningful to me because it teaches such a simple and powerful way to live a faithful life. His invitation to fill our minds with truth, fill our lives with service, and fill our hearts with love made me reflect on how I want to grow as a disciple of Jesus Christ. One part that touched me deeply was the story of Hank Aaron and the sacrifice his father made so he could continue his education. That small but powerful act showed how love is often expressed through quiet sacrifices and the example parents set for their children. I was also moved by the testimony that Jesus Christ is more than a teacher. He is the Savior and Redeemer who showed us the perfect way to live through truth, service, and love. This message inspired me to purposefully follow that pattern in my daily life and to strive each day to live in a way that draws me closer to the Savior.

“Jesus is more than a teacher. Jesus is the Savior of the world. He is the Redeemer of all mankind. He is the Son of God. He showed the way. You may recall that Jesus filled his mind with truth; Jesus filled his life with service; Jesus filled his heart with love. When”

— Thomas S. Monson

Life, Liberty and the Pursuit of Happiness

The reading made me think a lot about how we often chase the things, like money, status, or momentary pleasures, believing they will make us happy. It reminded me that happiness is something we are already born with, but true fulfillment comes from living a meaningful life. The idea of the Hero’s Journey really stood out to me. It is about using our talents to face challenges, helping others, building deep relationships, and connecting with something bigger than ourselves. I realized that a life well lived is not measured by wealth or comfort, but by growth, service, gratitude, and purpose. This reading encouraged me to pay more attention to the small choices I make every day, how I spend my time, who I invest in, and how I can give back, so that my life can have lasting meaning rather than just short-term pleasure.

“Once you have abandoned a foolish quest for the ephemeral rewards of happiness, wealth and power, you can begin to look for your true calling on this earth — a Hero’s Journey.”

Attitude on Money

Reading Attitude on Money was so interesting and gave me a lot to think about regarding how we view and handle money in our lives. I used to hear as a child that money was bad and that happiness could not be bought with it. Many religious teachings even suggested that rich people might not inherit the kingdom of God. I have come to realize that this is a misunderstanding. It is not money itself that is good or evil, but where the heart is and how money is used. Money has great power. It can provide for our families, support education, help the sick, and allow us to serve others, but it can also be misused if it becomes an obsession or is handled poorly. I loved how the author shows that to build wonderful temples and support church programs, money is necessary because it is people who make these things possible. The reading reminded me that our attitudes toward money are shaped by beliefs, experiences, and teachings we receive from a young age, and part of growing up is deciding which of these beliefs we accept and which we question. I want to be intentional about how I earn, manage, and use money so that it contributes to meaningful purposes rather than just temporary pleasures. I also want to continue learning how to be financially responsible and self-reliant while using my resources to bless others, provide opportunities, and make a positive difference in the world. This reading encouraged me to reflect on the choices I make each day, the priorities I set, and the ways I can align my financial decisions with my values and long-term goals. It helped me see that wealth is most valuable when it is paired with service, education, and gratitude and that developing good habits and a thoughtful perspective on money can lead to a life that is both prosperous and meaningful.

“With money we can build, we can educate, we can feed, we can serve; it is the use of money that matters, not the money itself.”

— Stephen W. Gibson, Attitude on Money

Is Work/Life Balance Possible?

In the video, Ann Miura-Ko talks about the challenges of balancing work and family, especially for entrepreneurs at the seed stage, where it can feel like life or death. She explains that balance is not always about perfection, but about making choices we truly believe are worth it. I found this perspective really meaningful because it reminded me that struggling to manage work and family is normal. Personally, I chose to pause my career to focus on my family. While I do miss the freedom that comes with financial independence, I feel grateful for the support of my spouse and the opportunity to continue developing my talents, pursuing my studies, and working toward my dreams. This video helped me see that even when life feels overwhelming, being intentional, passionate, and purposeful in my choices can make this season meaningful and the sacrifices worthwhile.

"Every day you’re making the choice to leave your family to go to work. If you don’t believe it’s worth it, you’re going to have a crisis of conscience at some point."

— Ann Miura-Ko, Floodgate

Balancing Your Life and Your Career Successfully

Randy Komisar’s talk about balancing life and career offered a powerful reminder that a truly balanced life is not about perfection or having everything at once. He shares that during the time he was hungry for experience, learning, and exploring life, even without making any money, he felt immense satisfaction and happiness. Later, when he worked as a CEO, earning money, gaining power, and achieving what many consider success, he realized he was not as happy. Komisar emphasizes that balance is dynamic, shaped by the choices we make and the trade-offs we accept between money, time, and personal fulfillment. I was struck by his insight that pursuing wealth or status alone does not guarantee satisfaction, and that experiences, passions, and relationships often provide the deepest sense of meaning. His teaching encourages reflection on what really matters in each stage of life and challenges me to intentionally evaluate my priorities, ask hard questions, and surround myself with people whose values I respect. The personal lesson I take from his words is that balance comes from clarity, conscious decisions, and living in alignment with one’s values rather than trying to meet society’s expectations.

“Balance is not a static state. It comes from clarity, conscious choices, and understanding what truly matters in life.”

— Randy Komisar

Hero's Journey Corey Bell

Corey Bell’s story shows the importance of having a clear vision for your life and having the courage to follow it. After working for several companies, he realized that his job did not align with the life he wanted to build, so he decided to start his own business, Tri-Fusion. Bell believes that entrepreneurship is about removing the word “can’t,” facing challenges with determination, and creating opportunities that can positively impact the lives of others. One part of his story that I found especially inspiring was when he shared that after his parents passed away, he and his wife took in his nine siblings and raised them while he was also running a business. I think that was incredibly brave and it must have been very challenging. His story shows great courage, responsibility, and love for family. Overall, his message reminds us that success is not only about business, but also about character, service, and making a meaningful difference in the lives of others.

“At the end of your life, no one’s really asking you about all the things that you did; they’re really concerned about who you are. I don’t want to live a life where I come to the end and I didn’t make an impact.”

— Corey Bell

Surviving the Entrepreneurial Life: Work and Family

Meg Cadoux Hirshberg’s message about balancing work and family offers so many practical and meaningful lessons. She emphasizes that while entrepreneurship demands being “all in,” strategies can be created to honor both business and family. I loved how she talked about attending children’s activities, giving them unstructured hangout time, and even involving them in the business to help them understand what you do. She also highlights the importance of maintaining connection with a spouse through small moments, setting boundaries with technology, and finding ways to engage together in shared projects or community service. What resonated most with me is her reminder that balance is not about perfection or rigid schedules but about consistently showing your family that they are your priority, while also helping children and spouses feel valued and included. This is a reminder that being intentional and thoughtful in both work and family life can create meaningful, lasting relationships.

“Every entrepreneur is going to face the challenge of how to balance the work-life equation. You matter most. Yes, my business is critical, but you matter more.”

— Meg Cadoux Hirshberg

Mini Cases

Reading these mini cases really made me reflect on the challenge of balancing work and family. The first case, about missing a child’s dance recital to handle a client emergency, hit me deeply. As a parent, keeping promises and being present for important milestones is very meaningful, but running a business also comes with serious responsibilities. If I were in that situation, I would do my best to handle both. I would make sure my child had support, perhaps from a grandparent, and explain afterward why I had to leave. Even if I could not be there the whole time, being fully present during the moments I do have can show love and strengthen our connection.

The second case, about David Hopkins, reminded me how career ambitions can affect family relationships over the long term. His passion and success were admirable, but his demanding career strained his marriage and his relationship with his daughter. These cases together made me realize that success is not just about achievements at work. It is also about love, trust, and connection with family. Life is full of compromises, and while I may not always have as much time with my children as I want, intentional, focused time even for just one hour can create meaningful connection and show them they are truly valued.
