Week 13- Journal Entry

An Attitude of Gratitude

“We can lift ourselves, and others as well, when we refuse to remain in the realm of negative thought and cultivate within our hearts an attitude of gratitude. If ingratitude be numbered among the serious sins, then gratitude takes its place among the noblest of virtues.”

— Thomas S. Monson

I love the message shared by Thomas S. Monson in his talk on gratitude. Gratitude is something I deeply value, and I try to be intentional about recognizing and appreciating the people around me. It matters not only to express thanks, but also to notice when others show gratitude, because it strengthens relationships and brings more kindness into everyday life. President Monson begins with the powerful story of the ten lepers from the The Bible, reminding us that while many receive blessings, only a few pause to give thanks. That simple moment teaches how meaningful it is to acknowledge the good we receive.

He also reminds us that gratitude should be part of every area of our lives. We can show appreciation for our mothers and fathers who sacrifice so much, for teachers who guide and inspire us, for our friends who support and uplift us, for our country and the freedoms we enjoy, and above all for our Savior, Jesus Christ. This talk is a beautiful reminder of the importance of expressing gratitude for those who take the time to help us, listen to us, and simply be there for us in our everyday lives. Gratitude is not just something we feel, but something we should actively show through our words and actions. When we pause to recognize the kindness and support we receive, we begin to see more clearly how blessed we truly are.

Identifying and Exploiting the Right Entrepreneurial Opportunity...For You

"The pursuit of opportunities is the essence of entrepreneurship. We define an opportunity as a future state that is both desirable and achievable."

— Identifying and Exploiting the Right Entrepreneurial Opportunity (Harvard Business School)

Reading about identifying and pursuing the right entrepreneurial opportunity reminded me that entrepreneurship is much more than following an exciting idea. A true opportunity is one that is not only financially realistic but also fits with our personal skills, values, and life goals. It is easy to be drawn to ideas that seem promising but do not match what we are capable of or what we want for our lives. What stood out to me is the importance of knowing ourselves, understanding our strengths and weaknesses, so we can focus on opportunities where we can make a real difference. Entrepreneurship is a continuous journey, and new ideas will always arise, requiring flexibility, careful evaluation, and a willingness to adapt along the way.

Another important lesson I learned is that entrepreneurship affects every part of our lives. Every choice impacts our relationships, time, and well-being, so it is crucial to find a balance between career ambitions and personal fulfillment. Planning ahead is helpful, not as a strict path to follow, but as a way to understand what matters most and guide our decisions thoughtfully. Taking risks and making sacrifices are part of the process, but when we evaluate opportunities carefully and stay aware of our priorities, it is possible to create ventures that are both successful and meaningful. Pursuing opportunities with intention and honesty allows us to build not just a business, but a life that feels purposeful.

Recognizing and Shaping Opportunities

“Chance favors only the prepared mind.”

— Louis Pasteur

Entrepreneurship is not simply about having a brilliant idea; it requires a “prepared mind” that can recognize opportunities and shape them into viable businesses. Part 1 explains that just like scientists use experience and structured methods to make discoveries, successful entrepreneurs use their knowledge, networks, and observations to see patterns others might miss. An idea becomes an opportunity when it is paired with a business model that identifies a target market, meets a need, and creates value for customers, investors, and other stakeholders. The examples of Leo Fernández, Robin Chase, Antje Danielson, and Evan Williams show how entrepreneurs turn personal insights and experiences into ventures, constantly testing and refining their ideas to meet real market demands.

From reading sections 2.1 and 2.2, I learned that recognizing opportunities requires both intuition and analytical thinking. Entrepreneurs observe trends, question assumptions, experiment, and network with others to develop and validate their ideas. Motivation and creative thinking are critical, as they drive persistence through uncertainty and failures. I found it inspiring that these founders combined their expertise, curiosity, and determination to transform small sparks of ideas into successful ventures like Telepizza, Zipcar, and Twitter. This taught me that entrepreneurship is an iterative process that blends preparation, insight, and action rather than relying on luck or sudden inspiration.

Paths to an Entrepreneurial Career

“Go start a company. It’s not a bad idea to go work for a company and then go fuel a company’s growth.”

— Ted Zoller

Ted Zoller teaches that there is no single path to becoming a successful entrepreneur or dealmaker, and the journey often takes unexpected routes. While many assume that starting in investment banking or launching multiple businesses as a serial entrepreneur is the most effective approach, his research shows that rising to leadership roles within large companies and then moving into high-growth or private ventures is often the most common path. Along the way, consulting and other professional experiences can provide valuable skills, knowledge, and networks that can later be leveraged to launch or support entrepreneurial ventures. The main lesson I take from this is that entrepreneurial success is built not just by ideas or risk-taking, but by gaining diverse experiences, observing patterns in the market, and strategically preparing for opportunities. It reminds me that learning from established organizations, connecting with people, and building expertise can be just as important as starting a business immediately, and these steps can ultimately strengthen and accelerate an entrepreneurial career.

Avoid the Wrong Job

“The most important criteria when thinking about how to choose a job is pick something you think is fun, that you're going to enjoy. You're going to prosper, do well in things that you're having fun with and so it isn't about sort of pain.”

— Stan Christensen

Stan Christensen explains that many people end up in the wrong career because they make assumptions that are not helpful. He emphasizes that you should not choose jobs just to impress others or follow what you think they want; instead, focus on what you genuinely enjoy and feel passionate about. Your first job does not have to determine your entire career because you will likely try several roles and move around, and that is normal. Choosing work that is interesting and fun allows you to grow, do well, and succeed even if some parts of the job are challenging. Christensen also warns against over-specializing in a single skill because skills can become outdated or irrelevant over time. Instead, developing a broad set of skills and experiences gives you flexibility to take advantage of new opportunities and find roles that are both meaningful and enjoyable.

Acton Hero Larry North

“I could have the worst day of my life and I wake up with a good attitude every day. It means that you just believe in yourself, you believe in the people you’re around, you believe in your family, you believe in your friends, you believe in your community and that’s been the single biggest asset that I’ve had.”

— Larry North

I really enjoyed listening to Larry North’s story. He came from a very difficult situation where his father was an alcoholic and a compulsive gambler, which made his childhood unstable and challenging. From a young age, he wanted to be in a healthy environment and worked hard to create a better life for himself. Despite these obstacles, he sought out good mentors and tried to learn as much as he could from them, even though he admitted that he didn’t always follow their advice at first. I was inspired by his resilience and determination, as he didn’t let failures stop him but used them as lessons to improve and grow. His story showed me the importance of having a positive attitude, believing in yourself and the people around you, and using every experience, whether success or failure, as an opportunity to become stronger and wiser. It reminded me that becoming successful often takes patience, persistence, and the courage to keep going even when the path is difficult.

Randy Haykin: The Making of an Entrepreneur (A)

This week I had the opportunity to study the Randy Haykin case, and it gave me a clear understanding of how curiosity, learning, and resilience shape a career. I learned that being a lifelong learner is not just about gaining knowledge but also about adapting, solving problems creatively, and taking on challenges with confidence. Haykin’s experience at Apple, where he explored projects beyond his assignments, showed how new skills can prepare you for future ventures. One of the most memorable moments was when he had to shut down a start-up and tell his team they would lose their jobs. He handled it with honesty and care, which taught me that leadership is about integrity and empathy as much as success. Entrepreneurship comes with risks like financial uncertainty and long hours, but Haykin’s story shows that pursuing meaningful work and helping others grow can make those risks worthwhile. I also learned the value of flexibility, stepping into unfamiliar situations, and balancing personal life with professional goals. Overall, this case gave me practical lessons on adaptability, leadership, and thoughtful risk-taking, leaving me motivated to continue learning, growing, and approaching opportunities with purpose.
